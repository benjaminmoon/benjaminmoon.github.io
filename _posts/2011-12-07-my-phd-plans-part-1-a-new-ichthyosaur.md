<p>Well, having given a presentation on the plans for my PhD, I feel as though I can now share it with the world.  That and I’ve had agreement on what I plan to do and how I plan to do.  Thus, here is an edited pseudo-transcript (mostly made up) on my PhD plans for the next year or so.  This will be in the form of three posts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Part 1: a new ichthyosaur</li>
<li><a title="My PhD: Part 2: the ichthyosaurs strike back" href="http://ichthyosaurs.wordpress.com/2011/12/13/my-phd-part-2-the-ichthyosaurs-strike-back/">Part 2: the ichthyosaurs strike back</a></li>
<li><a title="My PhD: Part 3: the return of the ichthyosaurs" href="http://ichthyosaurs.wordpress.com/2012/01/05/my-phd-part-3-the-return-of-the-ichthyosaurs/">Part 3: the return of the ichthyosaurs</a></li>
</ul>
<p>My thesis is based upon an unpublished thesis of Angela Kirton (1983).  As it stands, it is currently divided into two main projects.  These will certainly lead on to several further projects.  The initial part is to redescribe the three genera (group of species) of Late Jurassic ichthyosaurs from Great Britain.  Concurrent with this, I will be comparing the three main phylogenetic analyses from 1999<em>a</em> (Motani) and 2000 (Maisch and Matzke; Sander).</p>
<p><strong>Summary</strong></p>
<p>As these posts will be quite long, I will put a small summary at the beginning of each.  This way you can get the gist and come back to the details later.</p>
<p>The first part of my PhD will be to describe the three genera of Late Jurassic ichthyosaurs from Britain:</p>
<ul>
<ul>
<li><em>Ophthalmosaurus</em></li>
<li><em>Brachypterygius</em></li>
<li><em>Nannopterygius</em></li>
</ul>
<li>These can be identified based upon features of the skull and forelimb:
<ul>
<li><em>Ophthalmosaurus</em> has a very large eye</li>
<li><em>Brachypterygius</em> has three equal-sized facets on the humerus</li>
<li><em>Nannopterygius</em> has very tiny paddles</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Ichthyosaur basics</strong></p>
<p>[Specimen abbreviations: BMNH—Natural History Museum, London (originally British Museum (Natural History))]</p>
<p>There are a few small things that I must clear up and add from my ‘Introduction to ichthyosaurs’ blog.  Firstly, how we describe different parts of vertebrates, and where the features of interest are (fig. 1).</p>
<p>The front of the animal (where the head is) is called anterior.  The rear, or tail end, is posterior: your hands are anterior to your legs.  The belly-side is ventral whereas the back is dorsal: your spine is dorsal to your belly button.  Each side is called the right or left lateral side: your right hand is on the right lateral side.  Closer to the body is proximal whereas distal is farther: your hand is distal to your elbow.</p>
<p>[caption id="attachment_76" align="alignright" width="584" caption="Fig. 1 Reconstruction of Ophthalmosaurus icenicus (length about 3.3 m; modified from Kirton 1983)."]<a href="http://ichthyosaurs.files.wordpress.com/2011/12/ophthalmosaurus-icenicus-reconstruction.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-76" title="Ophthalmosaurus icenicus reconstruction" src="{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/ophthalmosaurus-icenicus-reconstruction.jpg" alt="" width="584" height="162" /></a>[/caption]</p>
<p>The ichthyosaurs that I am looking at show different features of the skull and paddle.  In the skull, the size of the eye is the most obvious difference.  The forepaddle (arm) varies in relative size, as do the number and size of the distal facets (concave faces) on the humerus (fig. 2).</p>
<p>[caption id="attachment_75" align="alignleft" width="350" caption="Fig. 2 Right forepaddle of Ophthalmosaurus icenicus from the Oxford Clay of Peterborough (BMNH R3702; length about 650 mm) in dorsal view. Note the three facets on the distal end of the humerus and the large number of digits (arab numerals) and phalanges (roman numerals). The anterior- and posteriormost digits are accessory digits (modified from Kirton 1983)."]<a href="http://ichthyosaurs.files.wordpress.com/2011/12/ophthalmosaurus-bmnh-r3702-rfpd.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-75 " title="Ophthalmosaurus BMNH R3702 RfpD" src="{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/ophthalmosaurus-bmnh-r3702-rfpd.jpg" alt="" width="350" height="742" /></a>[/caption]</p>
<p>Ichthyosaurs can have many digits (fingers) and phalanges (finger bones).  Humans have five digits on each hand, each with three phalanges (the thumb has two).  Some ichthyosaurs could have eight digits, each with over 20 phalanges.  Oddly enough, these are not all the same as our five fingers.  <em>Ophthalmosaurus</em> (fig. 2) has six digits and up to nine phalanges on each.  The middle four digits (fig. 2, numbered) are analogous to ours.  However, the other two are ‘accessory digits’ from different origins.  These definitions will be important in describing the ichthyosaurs below.</p>
<p><strong>British Upper Jurassic ichthyosaurs</strong></p>
<p>The three ichthyosaur genera from the British Late Jurassic are: <em>Ophthalmosaurus</em>, <em>Brachypterygius</em> and <em>Nannopterygius</em>.<em> </em> I will give the most visible differences of these.</p>
<p><strong><em>Ophthalmosaurus</em></strong> (oph-THAL-mo-SORE-us) is found in the Oxford Clay of the Callovian and Oxfordian stages (~162–~155 Ma).  Some possible remains are from the Kimmeridgian Stage (~155.6–150.8 Ma).  As the name suggests, this ichthyosaur is famous for having a large eye (‘ophthalmo-’ is from the Latin for eye; fig. 3).  It is the largest eye compared to body size known (Motani 1999<em>b</em>)!  The humerus has three distal facets.  These articulate with the pre-axial accessory digit, the radius and the ulna (Seeley 1874; Kirton 1983; McGowan and Motani 2003).  The anterior facet is the smallest.  The posteriormost is oblique to the other two.</p>
<p><strong><em><br />
</em></strong></p>
<p>[caption id="attachment_77" align="alignright" width="584" caption="Fig. 3 Skull of Ophthalmosaurus icenicus showing the very large orbit, where the eye sits (based upon BMNH R3893 &amp; R 4753; length about 800 mm). The ring of bones (sclerotic ring) would have kept the shape of the eye and assisted in focussing underwater. Modified from McGowan and Motani (2003)."]<a href="http://ichthyosaurs.files.wordpress.com/2011/12/ophthalmosaurus-mcg-mot-2003-sk.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-77" title="Ophthalmosaurus McG Mot 2003 Sk" src="{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/ophthalmosaurus-mcg-mot-2003-sk.jpg" alt="" width="584" height="178" /></a>[/caption]</p>
<p><em>Ophthalmosaurus icenicus</em>, the single British species, is the most common Late Jurassic ichthyosaur.  Many specimens have been found near Peterborough, collected by the Leeds brothers in the last half of the 19<sup>th</sup> Century.  The Leeds’ Collection was largely donated to the Natural History Museum in London, but parts went to museums around the UK.</p>
<p><strong><em>Brachyterygius</em> </strong>(BRACK-ip-ter-I-gi-us)<strong> </strong>is known from the Kimmeridgian (~155.6–150.8 Ma) of Dorset but there are also some possible finds from the Purbeck Beds (Lower Cretaceous, ~143 Ma) (Ensom <em>et al.</em> 2009) and even later (Lydekker 1888; McGowan and Motani 2003).  This genus was originally described from a forepaddle (fig. 4).  <em>Brachypterygius</em>’s paddle also has three distal facets on the humerus, each is subequally sized and oblique to each other (McGowan 1997).  These facets articulate (anterior to posterior) with the radius, (unusually) intermedium and ulna.  As with <em>Ophthalmosaurus</em> there are four digits with one pre- and one post-axial accessory digit.</p>
<p>[caption id="attachment_73" align="alignleft" width="350" caption="Fig. 4 Brachypterygius extremes forepaddle showing the three equally sized distal humeral facets articulating with the radius, intermedium and ulna. Specimen BMNH R3177 (length about 400 mm; modified from Kirton 1983)."]<a href="http://ichthyosaurs.files.wordpress.com/2011/12/brachypterygius-bmnh-r3177-rfpd.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-73 " title="Brachypterygius BMNH R3177 RfpD" src="{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/brachypterygius-bmnh-r3177-rfpd.jpg" alt="" width="350" height="599" /></a>[/caption]</p>
<p>A skull in the Sedgwick Museum, Cambridge, described as <em>Grendelius</em>, was suggested to be <em>Brachypterygius</em>.  This was confirmed by a specimen in Bristol Museum.  This whole body specimen posses the skull of <em>Grendelius</em> with the forepaddle of <em>Brachypterygius </em>(McGowan 1997).  Because of this, <em>Grendelius</em> was renamed <em>Brachypterygius</em> (the earlier name gets precedence).  There are two species of <em>Brachypterygius </em>from the British Late Jurassic: <em>B. extremus</em> (the original) and <em>B. mordax</em> (originally Grendelius; McGowan 1997).</p>
<p><strong><em>Nannopterygius</em> </strong>(NAN-op-ter-I-gi-us) is the most uncommon, and unusual, of British Late Jurassic ichthyosaurs.  Only one whole body specimen, and one forepaddle, is known.  As the ‘nanno-’ in the name suggests, there is something small on this ichthyosaur: the paddles (see fig. 5; Hulke 1871).  <em>Nannopterygius</em> has significantly reduced fore- and hindpaddles (McGowan and Motani 2003).  These contain less than half the number of bones of other ichthyosaurs.  The humerus of <em>Nannopterygius</em> is also different from <em>Ophthalmosaurus</em> and <em>Brachypterygius</em>: it has only two distal facets.  These articulate with the radius and ulna (McGowan and Motani 2003).  The single British species, <em>Nannopterygius enthekiodon</em>, is from the Kimmeridgian of Kimmeridge Bay, Dorset (Hulke 1871).</p>

<p><strong>Rationale</strong></p>
<p>Whereas <em>Ophthalmosaurus</em> has a lot of material available, <em>Brachypterygius</em> and <em>Nannopterygius</em> are known from only a few good specimens each.  These were described when first found, in the late 19<sup>th</sup> and early 20<sup>th</sup> centuries, but briefly, and have escaped extended descriptions since.  The largest recent work was done by Angela Kirton (1983) in her PhD thesis but is unpublished.  The recent surge in ichthyosaur interest, particularly in this millennium, has meant that the need for definite and accurate descriptions has increased.  This is especially important for conducting phylogenetic analyses, which is what I will talk about in my next post.</p>
